# Disc Golf Discs

Terminal-friendly cheat sheet. Last updated 2026-07-23.

## Quick rules

- Flight numbers are **Speed / Glide / Turn / Fade**.
- Numbers describe an intended flight, not a guarantee.
- Compare numbers mainly within the same speed class and
  manufacturer.
- Faster does not automatically mean farther.
- Premium plastic is a good default for throwers.
- Grippy baseline plastic is a good default for putting putters.
- Weight, wear, plastic, wind, elevation, nose angle, and release
  angle matter.

Directions below assume a right-handed backhand (**RHBH**). Reverse
left and right for a right-handed forehand or left-handed backhand.

## Flight numbers

Example: a Buzzz is commonly marked **5 / 4 / -1 / 1**.

|  \# | Name  | Meaning                                     |
|----:|-------|---------------------------------------------|
|   1 | Speed | Required launch speed; related to rim width |
|   2 | Glide | Tendency to remain aloft                    |
|   3 | Turn  | High-speed movement right for RHBH          |
|   4 | Fade  | Low-speed finish left for RHBH              |

### Speed classes

| Speed | Typical class              |
|------:|----------------------------|
|   1-4 | Putt and approach          |
|   4-6 | Midrange                   |
|   6-9 | Fairway driver             |
|  9-11 | Control/distance driver    |
| 11-14 | High-speed distance driver |

### Throw directions

| Throw | Turn  | Fade  |
|-------|-------|-------|
| RHBH  | Right | Left  |
| RHFH  | Left  | Right |
| LHBH  | Left  | Right |
| LHFH  | Right | Left  |

### Stability

- **Understable:** turns more easily and finishes less strongly.
  Useful for beginners, hyzer-flips, turnovers, rollers, tailwinds,
  and lower-power throws.
- **Stable or neutral:** holds its release line with modest turn and
  fade. "Stable" does not necessarily mean "overstable."
- **Overstable:** resists turn and fades reliably. Useful for
  headwinds, power, forehands, hyzers, flex shots, and skips.
- **Very overstable or utility:** produces an aggressive, dependable
  finish rather than maximum straight-line distance.

Stability is relative to the thrower. A Destroyer may be overstable
for a 300-foot thrower and workable or flippy for an elite-power
thrower.

### Discraft's extra number

Some Discraft discs also show one stability number, such as **0.5**
or **2.0**. It is separate from the four-number system:

- negative = understable;
- near zero = straight;
- larger positive = more overstable.

Use it only as an additional within-Discraft reference.

## Weight

| Class    | Default   | Easier    | Power/wind |
|----------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Putter   | 170-175 g | 165-170 g | 173-175 g  |
| Approach | 170-175 g | 165-170 g | Near max   |
| Midrange | 175-180 g | 165-174 g | Near max   |
| Fairway  | 168-175 g | 155-167 g | 172-175 g  |
| Distance | 168-173 g | 150-167 g | 173-175 g  |

- Lighter discs accelerate more easily and may turn or travel
  farther.
- Heavier discs usually resist wind and unwanted turn better.
- Maximum legal weight depends on diameter. Drivers often max near
  175 g; wider mids may be legal near 180 g.
- Actual weight can differ slightly from the marked retail range.

For a player with a strong drive:

- mids: **175-180 g**;
- fairways: **170-175 g**;
- distance: **168-173 g** for workability;
- distance: **173-175 g** for wind and turn resistance.

## Plastic fundamentals

Plastic changes:

- grip and release;
- stiffness and flexibility;
- durability and rate of wear;
- dome and parting-line height after cooling;
- sometimes turn, fade, glide, and skip.

Premium plastic keeps its flight longer. Baseline and midgrade
plastic generally season faster, becoming straighter and then more
understable.

No rule such as "plastic X is always more overstable" is universal.
Mold, weight, run, color, cooling, wear, and individual disc shape
can override the usual tendency.

## Discraft plastics

These are the labels commonly shown at Disc Store.

### Big Z

Opaque, solid-color premium Z-family plastic, often with a large
stamp. Durable and commonly more tactile than clear Z. Choose it
when regular Z feels too slick but long-lasting flight is still
important.

### Elite X / X Line

**Elite X is the old name for X Line.** It is grippy midgrade
plastic with good value, but it wears faster than ESP or Z. Choose
it for lower cost, cold/wet grip, or a disc that should season into
turnovers.

### Elite Z / Z Line

**Elite Z is the old name for Z Line.** It is firm, commonly
translucent, and among Discraft's most durable plastics. Choose it
for a long-lasting flight, warm weather, or additional turn
resistance.

An authentically old Elite Z run may feel different because blends
and aging vary. Ordinary current retailer listings usually mean
stock Z Line.

### Elite Z Fly Dye

Z-family plastic with a unique dye pattern. Performance is broadly
the same as Z; the dye is mainly cosmetic. Complex or dark dyes can
be harder to find on the course.

### ESP

Discraft's balanced premium workhorse: opaque, durable, grippy, and
usually moderately flexible. It is roughly analogous to Innova Star.

Discraft now treats **ESP** as the product-family name rather than
prominently spelling it out. Retailers often expand it as "Elite
Special Plastic."

### ESP FLX

A softer, more flexible ESP. It retains premium durability while
adding tacky grip. Good for cold or wet weather, softer landings,
and players who dislike stiff discs.

### Recycled ESP

Discraft's recycled premium blend. It has a tacky or comfortable
grip and a durable feel, usually at a lower price than ESP.
Appearance, stiffness, and run feel may vary more.

### Swirly ESP

A swirled cosmetic/run variation of ESP. Expect premium ESP-like
grip and durability, but treat exact stiffness and flight as
run-dependent.

### Titanium / Titanium Swirl

Dense-feeling, highly durable premium plastic, often with a metallic
or pearly appearance. Titanium Swirl adds swirled color. Ti
formulations have changed, so current and older runs may not feel
identical.

### Z FLX

A flexible, gummy Z-family plastic with high durability and
all-weather grip. It tends to skip, kick, and bounce less than stiff
Z. Good for approaches, woods, cold weather, and wet weather.

### Other common Discraft labels

- **Jawbreaker:** very grippy putt/approach plastic. Seasons faster
  than premium blends.
- **Putter Line:** baseline putter plastic offered in multiple
  stiffnesses.
- **Z Lite:** lightweight Z-family plastic for easier acceleration.
- **FLX:** a softer, more flexible version of the named plastic
  family.
- **Swirl, Glo, Sparkle, CryZtal, and Tour Series:** special
  appearance or blend variations. Feel and flight can be
  run-specific.

## Why identical discs differ

- **Parting-line height (PLH):** on otherwise comparable discs, a
  higher wing edge often indicates more overstability.
- **Dome:** more dome can add glide. Flat discs may feel cleaner for
  forehands. The stability effect depends on the mold.
- **Run and cooling:** small manufacturing and cooling differences
  change final geometry.
- **Wear:** impacts reduce stability over time, especially in
  baseline and midgrade plastic.
- **Color and additives:** pigments and special additives can subtly
  alter feel and cooling.

When replacing a favorite driver, prefer a retailer that photographs
and weighs the exact disc over a random color/weight listing.

## Wind, elevation, and weather

- **Headwind:** raises effective airspeed and exposes more turn. Use
  something heavier or more overstable and keep the nose down.
- **Tailwind:** lowers effective airspeed and makes a disc act more
  stable. Use something glidier, lighter, or more understable.
- **Crosswind:** can lift or push the exposed underside. Hyzer and
  anhyzer angles determine whether wind gets under the flight plate.
- **High elevation:** thin air generally produces less turn, less
  glide, and a more overstable-looking flight. In Colorado, consider
  slightly lighter or more understable discs than at sea level.
- **Cold or rain:** cold makes many plastics firmer. FLX or gummy
  blends improve grip. In rain, hand feel and a dry towel often
  matter more than theoretical stability.

## Release angles and shots

- **Hyzer:** outside edge angled down. Produces a curving finish.
- **Flat:** flight plate released roughly level.
- **Anhyzer:** outside edge angled up. Encourages turn.
- **Hyzer-flip:** an understable disc starts on hyzer, flips to flat,
  and may continue turning.
- **Turnover:** turns and holds the opposite-curving line without
  necessarily fading back.
- **Flex shot:** an overstable disc starts on anhyzer, turns, then
  fights back with fade.
- **Roller:** intentionally turned onto its edge to roll.

Nose angle is critical. A nose-up driver climbs, stalls, and fades
early even when its mold and weight are appropriate.

## Geometry and hand feel

- **Rim width:** wider generally means faster, but may be
  uncomfortable for smaller hands.
- **Rim depth:** deep putters may glide well but can feel slower to
  release.
- **Bead:** ridge along the rim's bottom. It changes hand feel;
  neither beaded nor beadless is inherently better.
- **Dome:** raised flight plate, often associated with glide.
- **Flat top:** often preferred for controlled forehands and clean
  releases.
- **Puddle top:** center sits below the shoulder; common in some
  approach discs and putters.

Hand feel is decisive for putting putters. Choose what releases
cleanly, then buy several matching copies for practice.

## Major manufacturers

There is no universally best manufacturer. A mixed-brand bag is
practical.

- **Innova:** huge catalog and plastic selection; Aviar, Teebird,
  Firebird, Wraith, Destroyer.
- **Discraft:** strong mids and approaches; Buzzz, Zone, Comet,
  Undertaker, Heat.
- **MVP / Axiom / Streamline:** premium plastics and gyro options;
  Envy, Hex, Crave, Trail, Wave, Pixel.
- **Latitude 64 / Dynamic / Westside:** comfortable plastics and
  many glide-forward molds; Pure, River, Judge, Felon, Harp.
- **Discmania:** control-oriented lineup; P2, FD, MD3, DD3.
- **Kastaplast:** tactile plastics; Berg, Reko, Lots, Falk.
- **Gateway:** putter specialist with many stiffnesses; especially
  Wizard.
- **Infinite:** wide lineup and value blends; Alpaca, Tomb, Emperor,
  Pharaoh, Sphinx.
- **Prodigy, DGA, Clash, Thought Space, Mint, RPM:** reputable
  alternatives; shop by flight and feel rather than logo.

#### Buzzz family

- **Buzzz:** neutral default.
- **Buzzz SS:** understable; turnovers, hyzer-flips, and lower
  power.
- **Buzzz OS:** overstable; wind, forehands, and hard fade.
- Start with ESP.
- Choose Z for a firmer, extremely durable option.
- Choose ESP FLX or Z FLX for flexibility.
- Choose X only when faster seasoning is desirable.

## Compact, versatile bag

1.  Putting putter chosen by hand feel.
2.  Envy or another neutral throwing putter.
3.  Zone for overstable approaches.
4.  Buzzz for neutral midrange shots.
5.  Crave or Teebird for straight/stable fairway shots.
6.  Firebird for wind and utility.
7.  Wraith for workable distance.
8.  Destroyer only when the extra speed produces a useful flight.

Add an understable mold only when a turnover, hyzer-flip, roller, or
tailwind slot is missing.

## Buying checklist

- Confirm the exact mold, including SS, OS, 3, L, or other suffixes.
- Confirm the plastic; product pages often group several variants.
- Confirm an exact weight or narrow range.
- Prefer bright pink, orange, blue, or white throwers.
- Avoid green, brown, black, or camouflage-like dyes when visibility
  matters.
- Prefer exact-disc photos and scaled weights online.
- Amazon often randomizes color and groups variants.
- Verify PDGA approval before sanctioned competition.
- Record mold, plastic, weight, and purchase date.
- Field-test new discs in calm conditions.
