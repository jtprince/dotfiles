# Disc Golf Discs Cheat Sheet

Last updated: 2026-07-23

## The short version

- Flight numbers are **Speed / Glide / Turn / Fade**.
- Numbers describe an intended flight, not a guarantee. They are most useful
  when comparing similar-speed discs from the same manufacturer.
- For a right-handed backhand (RHBH), negative turn moves right early and fade
  moves left late. Reverse those directions for left-handed backhand (LHBH) or
  right-handed forehand (RHFH).
- Faster discs do not automatically fly farther. A disc that is too fast for
  the thrower usually fades early and loses distance.
- Plastic, weight, wear, dome, parting-line height, wind, elevation, nose angle,
  spin, and release angle can matter as much as the printed numbers.
- A practical default is premium plastic for throwers and grippy baseline
  plastic for putting putters.
- Do not buy by brand loyalty alone. Most established manufacturers make
  excellent discs.

## Flight numbers

Example: a Discraft Buzzz is commonly marked **5 / 4 / -1 / 1**.

| Position | Name | What it means | Practical interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Speed | How fast the disc is designed to move; strongly related to rim width | Higher speed requires more launch speed and clean form |
| 2 | Glide | The disc's tendency to remain aloft | More glide can add effortless distance but may reduce wind predictability |
| 3 | Turn | High-speed movement early in flight | More negative = easier rightward movement for RHBH |
| 4 | Fade | Low-speed finish as the disc slows | Higher = stronger left finish for RHBH |

Typical speed classes:

| Class | Approximate speed |
|---|---:|
| Putt and approach | 1-4 |
| Midrange | 4-6 |
| Fairway driver | 6-9 |
| Control/distance driver | 9-11 |
| High-speed distance driver | 11-14 |

### Turn and fade directions

| Throw | Turn during high-speed flight | Fade during low-speed flight |
|---|---|---|
| Right-handed backhand (RHBH) | Right | Left |
| Right-handed forehand (RHFH) | Left | Right |
| Left-handed backhand (LHBH) | Left | Right |
| Left-handed forehand (LHFH) | Right | Left |

### Stability vocabulary

- **Understable:** turns more easily and/or finishes less strongly. Useful for
  beginners, hyzer-flips, turnovers, rollers, tailwinds, and low-power throws.
- **Stable/neutral:** holds the release line with modest turn and fade. In disc
  golf, "stable" does not necessarily mean "overstable."
- **Overstable:** strongly resists turn and fades reliably. Useful for
  headwinds, power throws, forehands, hyzers, flex shots, and skips.
- **Very overstable/utility:** designed for an aggressive, dependable finish,
  not maximum straight-line distance.

The terms are relative to the thrower. A Destroyer can be overstable for a
300-foot thrower and workable or even flippy for an elite-power thrower.

### Discraft's extra stability number

Some Discraft listings also show one number such as **0.5** or **2.0**. This is
Discraft's older proprietary stability scale, separate from the four-number
system:

- Negative values are more understable.
- Values near zero are straighter.
- Larger positive values are more overstable.

Use the four flight numbers for the basic shape and the Discraft stability
number as an additional within-brand reference.

## Weight

Weight is marked in grams. It affects how easily a disc reaches its intended
speed, how it handles wind, and how much momentum it carries.

| Disc type | Common all-purpose range | Easier-distance range | Power/wind range |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Putting putter | 170-175 g | 165-170 g | 173-175 g |
| Throwing putter/approach | 170-175 g | 165-170 g | Near max weight |
| Midrange | 175-180 g | 165-174 g | Near max weight |
| Fairway driver | 168-175 g | 155-167 g | 172-175 g |
| Distance driver | 168-173 g | 150-167 g | 173-175 g |

General tendencies:

- Lighter discs are easier to accelerate and may produce more turn and
  distance, but wind moves them more easily.
- Heavier discs usually resist wind and unwanted turn better, but require more
  speed to fly as intended.
- Maximum legal weight depends on disc diameter. Many drivers max out around
  175 g; wider-diameter mids may be legal around 180 g.
- Actual measured weight can differ slightly from the marked retail range.

For a player with a strong drive, a useful starting point is:

- mids: 175-180 g;
- fairways: 170-175 g;
- distance drivers: 168-173 g for workable distance, or 173-175 g for wind and
  maximum turn resistance.

## Plastic: what changes

Plastic affects:

- grip and release;
- stiffness or flexibility;
- durability and rate of wear;
- cooling shape, dome, and parting-line height;
- sometimes turn, fade, glide, and skip.

Premium plastic keeps its flight longer. Baseline and midgrade plastic usually
beats in faster, becoming straighter and then more understable. No rule such as
"plastic X is always more overstable" is universal; mold, run, color, cooling,
weight, and wear can override the usual tendency.

### Discraft plastic names seen at Disc Store

| Disc Store label | What it is | Feel and durability | When to choose it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Big Z** | Opaque/solid-color premium Z-family plastic, often with a large stamp | Durable, typically a little more tactile than clear Z | Durable thrower when regular Z feels too slick |
| **Elite X** | Legacy name for **X Line** | Grippy midgrade plastic; less durable than ESP or Z; beats in relatively quickly | Lower cost, cold/wet grip, or a disc intended to season into turnovers |
| **Elite Z** | Legacy name for **Z Line** | Firm, often translucent, and among Discraft's most durable plastics | Long-lasting flight, warm weather, and turn resistance |
| **Elite Z Fly Dye** | Z/Elite Z with unique dye patterns | Essentially Z-family durability; the dye is mainly cosmetic | Same reasons as Z, when the appearance appeals |
| **ESP FLX** | Softer, more flexible ESP | Premium durability with tackier grip and more flex | Cold/wet weather, players who dislike stiff discs, softer landings |
| **Recycled ESP** | Discraft's recycled premium blend | Tacky/comfortable grip and durable feel; often cheaper; appearance and run feel can vary | Value and lower material impact without dropping to baseline plastic |
| **Swirly ESP** | A swirled cosmetic/run variation of ESP | Premium ESP-like grip and durability; exact stiffness and flight are run-dependent | ESP performance plus a distinctive appearance |
| **Titanium Swirl** | Swirled Titanium/Ti premium plastic | Dense premium feel, high durability, often tacky; exact blends have changed over time | Long-lasting thrower when Ti hand feel is preferred |
| **Z FLX** | Flexible Z-family plastic | Durable, gummy, all-weather grip; tends to skip, kick, and bounce less than stiff Z | Cold/wet play, approaches, woods, and reduced ground action |

Related standard plastics:

| Plastic | Summary |
|---|---|
| **ESP** | Discraft's balanced premium workhorse: durable, grippy, opaque, and usually moderately flexible |
| **Z Line** | Maximum-durability premium plastic; commonly firmer and translucent |
| **X Line** | Grip-and-value midgrade plastic that seasons faster |
| **Titanium (Ti)** | Durable premium blend with a metallic/pearly appearance and distinctive hand feel |
| **Jawbreaker** | Very grippy putt/approach plastic; seasons faster than premium blends |
| **Putter Line** | Baseline putter plastic available in multiple stiffnesses |
| **Z Lite** | Lightweight Z-family plastic for easier acceleration |

Discraft currently uses **ESP** as the product-family name rather than
prominently spelling it out. Retailers often expand it as "Elite Special
Plastic," but the useful meaning is simply Discraft's opaque, grippy premium
blend—the rough counterpart to Innova Star.

#### Important naming notes

- **Elite X = X Line** and **Elite Z = Z Line** in normal retail usage. "Elite"
  is the older name. A genuinely old collectible run may feel different because
  blends and aging change over time.
- **Fly Dye** describes the visual dye treatment, not a separate mold.
- **FLX** means a softer, more flexible version of the named family.
- **Swirl**, **Glo**, **Sparkle**, **CryZtal**, tour-series, and special-blend
  runs can differ from stock plastic. Treat run-specific claims as tendencies,
  not laws.

## Why identical models can fly differently

Two discs with the same mold, weight, plastic name, and flight numbers can
still differ because of:

- **Parting-line height (PLH):** on otherwise comparable discs, a higher wing
  edge often indicates more overstability.
- **Dome:** more dome can add glide; flat discs may feel cleaner for forehands.
  The stability effect is mold-dependent.
- **Run and cooling:** small manufacturing and cooling differences change the
  final geometry.
- **Wear:** impacts lower the effective stability over time, especially in
  baseline and midgrade plastic.
- **Color/additives:** pigments and special additives can subtly alter cooling
  and feel.

When replacing a favorite driver, buying from a retailer that photographs and
weighs the exact disc is safer than buying a random color/weight listing.

## Wind, elevation, and weather

### Wind

- **Headwind:** increases effective airspeed, exposing more turn. Prefer a
  heavier or more overstable disc and keep the nose down.
- **Tailwind:** reduces effective airspeed, making the disc act more stable.
  Prefer a glidier, lighter, or more understable disc.
- **Right-to-left crosswind:** can lift or push the exposed underside of a disc
  depending on hyzer/anhyzer angle.
- **Left-to-right crosswind:** creates the opposite risk.

### Elevation

At higher elevation, thinner air generally produces less turn, less glide, and
a more overstable-looking flight. A Colorado player may prefer a slightly
lighter or more understable disc than someone throwing the same distance near
sea level.

### Temperature and precipitation

- Cold makes many plastics feel firmer and can reduce grip; FLX or gummy blends
  help.
- Heat makes flexible plastics softer.
- In rain, hand feel and a clean towel often matter more than theoretical
  stability.

## Release angles and common shot shapes

- **Hyzer:** outside edge angled downward at release. Produces a curving finish.
- **Flat:** flight plate released roughly level.
- **Anhyzer:** outside edge angled upward. Encourages turn.
- **Hyzer-flip:** an understable disc starts on hyzer, flips to flat, and may
  continue turning.
- **Turnover:** disc turns and holds the opposite-curving line without
  necessarily fading back.
- **Flex shot:** an overstable disc is released on anhyzer, turns initially,
  then fights back with fade.
- **Roller:** disc is intentionally turned onto its edge to roll.

Nose angle is critical. A nose-up driver climbs, stalls, and fades early even
when the mold and weight are otherwise appropriate.

## Disc geometry and hand feel

- **Rim width:** wider rims generally correspond to higher speed but can be
  uncomfortable for smaller hands.
- **Rim depth:** deep putters may glide well but can feel slow to release.
- **Bead:** a ridge along the bottom of the rim. It changes hand feel and may
  influence wear/stability; neither beaded nor beadless is inherently better.
- **Dome:** raised flight plate; often associated with glide.
- **Flat top:** popular for controlled forehands and predictable releases.
- **Puddle top:** center sits lower than the shoulder; common in some approach
  discs and putters.

Hand feel is decisive for putting putters. Choose the mold and stiffness that
release cleanly, then buy several identical copies for practice.

## Major manufacturers and their strengths

There is no universally best manufacturer. Current fan preferences are broadly
split among several large families, and an open mixed-brand bag is usually the
most practical choice.

| Brand/family | Common strengths and notable molds |
|---|---|
| **Innova** | Huge catalog and plastic selection; benchmark drivers including Aviar, Teebird, Firebird, Wraith, and Destroyer |
| **Discraft** | Excellent mids and approaches; Buzzz, Zone, Comet, Undertaker, and Heat; broad plastic selection |
| **MVP / Axiom / Streamline** | Consistent premium plastics, gyro/overmold options, strong modern lineup; Envy, Hex, Crave, Trail, Wave, and Pixel |
| **Latitude 64 / Dynamic Discs / Westside** | Large coordinated lineup, comfortable premium plastics, many glide-forward molds; Pure, River, Explorer, Judge, Felon, Harp |
| **Discmania** | Control-oriented lineup and popular drivers/mids; P2, FD, MD3, DD3 |
| **Kastaplast** | Highly regarded tactile plastics and distinctive approach/putter molds; Berg, Reko, Lots, Falk |
| **Gateway** | Deep putter expertise and many stiffness options; especially the Wizard |
| **Infinite Discs** | Wide mold selection and popular value plastics; Alpaca, Tomb, Emperor, Pharaoh, Sphinx |
| **Prodigy, DGA, Clash, Thought Space, Mint, RPM** | Reputable alternatives with excellent individual molds; shop by flight and feel rather than logo |

## Consistently popular discs: useful starting specifications

These are conservative first-buy configurations for a typical experienced
adult player. They are not mandates. Adjust lighter for easier distance and
heavier/more overstable for power or wind.

### Putt and approach

| Disc | Flight | Role | Suggested first configuration |
|---|---:|---|---|
| **Discraft Zone** | 4 / 3 / 0 / 3 | Overstable approach and forehand utility | ESP or Z, 173-174 g |
| **Axiom Envy** | 3 / 3 / 0 / 2 | Stable throwing putter | Neutron, 170-175 g |
| **Gateway Wizard** | 2 / 3 / 0 / 2 | Beaded putting/throwing putter | Preferred baseline stiffness, 173-175 g |
| **Dynamic Discs Judge** | 2 / 4 / 0 / 1 | Beaded putting putter | Classic or Classic Blend, 173-176 g |
| **Innova Aviar** | 2 / 3 / 0 / 1 | Classic putting-putter family | DX Aviar P&A, 170-175 g; try the beaded KC/JK variants before buying multiples |
| **Axiom Pixel** | 2 / 4 / 0 / 0.5 | Straight putting/throwing putter | Electron Medium for putting, 170-175 g |

### Midranges

| Disc | Flight | Role | Suggested first configuration |
|---|---:|---|---|
| **Discraft Buzzz** | 5 / 4 / -1 / 1 | Neutral workhorse | **ESP, 175-177 g** |
| **Innova Mako3** | 5 / 5 / 0 / 0 | Very straight, angle-holding mid | Star, 175-180 g |
| **Innova Roc3** | 5 / 4 / 0 / 3 | Stable-to-overstable beaded mid | Champion, 175-180 g |
| **Axiom Hex** | 5 / 5 / -1 / 1 | Neutral workhorse alternative | Neutron, 175-178 g |

For a Buzzz:

- **Regular Buzzz:** neutral default.
- **Buzzz SS:** more understable; turnovers, hyzer-flips, lower power.
- **Buzzz OS:** overstable; wind, forehands, and hard fade.
- Start with ESP. Choose Z for a firmer, extremely durable option; ESP FLX or Z
  FLX for flexibility; X only when faster seasoning is desirable.

### Fairway and control drivers

| Disc | Flight | Role | Suggested first configuration |
|---|---:|---|---|
| **Innova Teebird** | 7 / 5 / 0 / 2 | Benchmark stable fairway | Star, 170-175 g |
| **Axiom Crave** | 6.5 / 5 / -1 / 1 | Straight, workable fairway | Neutron, 165-172 g |
| **Innova Thunderbird** | 9 / 5 / 0 / 2 | Stable control driver | Star, 170-175 g |
| **Innova Firebird** | 9 / 3 / 0 / 4 | Overstable utility/control driver | Champion, 173-175 g |

### Distance drivers

| Disc | Flight | Role | Suggested first configuration |
|---|---:|---|---|
| **Innova Wraith** | 11 / 5 / -1 / 3 | Workable distance for many experienced players | Star, 168-172 g |
| **Innova Destroyer** | 12 / 5 / -1 / 3 | Power distance and dependable fade | **Star, 170-173 g** |
| **MVP Trail** | 10 / 5 / -1 / 1 | Straight control distance | Neutron, 165-172 g |
| **Infinite Emperor** | 12 / 5 / -1 / 2.5 | Destroyer-like distance driver | I-Blend or S-Blend, 168-173 g |
| **Infinite Pharaoh** | 13 / 6 / -1 / 2 | Glide-forward maximum distance | S-Blend, 168-173 g |

For a first Destroyer:

- under about 325 feet of controlled distance: a Wraith, Trail, or lighter
  GStar/Pro Destroyer is usually more productive;
- about 325-375 feet: Star Destroyer around 168-172 g;
- about 375-425 feet: Star Destroyer around 170-175 g;
- 425+ feet or frequent headwinds: 173-175 g Star, Champion, or Halo Star.

Use controlled golf distance, not a one-time maximum throw, when selecting
speed and stability.

## A compact, versatile bag

A player does not need twenty molds. A capable core could be:

1. Putting putter chosen entirely by hand feel.
2. Axiom Envy or another neutral throwing putter.
3. Discraft Zone for overstable approaches.
4. Discraft Buzzz for neutral midrange shots.
5. Axiom Crave or Innova Teebird for straight/stable fairway shots.
6. Innova Firebird for wind and utility.
7. Innova Wraith for workable distance.
8. Innova Destroyer only when the extra speed produces a useful flight.

Add an understable disc only when a specific turnover, hyzer-flip, roller, or
tailwind slot is missing.

## Buying checklist

- Confirm the exact **mold**, including SS, OS, 3, L, or other suffixes.
- Confirm **plastic**; product families often group several plastics.
- Confirm an exact weight or narrow weight range.
- Prefer bright pink, orange, blue, or white throwers that are easy to find.
- Avoid green, brown, black, and elaborate camouflage-like dyes unless
  collectibility matters more than visibility.
- For online purchases, exact-disc photos and scaled weights are valuable.
- Amazon listings frequently randomize color and sometimes group variants.
- Check that the mold is on the [PDGA Approved Discs list](https://www.pdga.com/technical-standards/equipment-certification/discs)
  before sanctioned competition.
- Put a name and contact method on throwers if desired.
- Record the mold, plastic, marked/measured weight, and purchase date.
- Field-test new discs in calm conditions before trusting them in competition.
