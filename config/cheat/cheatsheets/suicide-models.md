## Legend

* ML   — *Machine Learning* (statistical prediction models) [~2010s]
  Data-driven approaches using large datasets (e.g., EHRs) to estimate risk via
  pattern recognition rather than theory. They consistently achieve the highest
  aggregate predictive performance (AUC ~0.7–0.85), largely by leveraging
  strong predictors like prior attempts and healthcare utilization. However,
  they suffer from base-rate problems, leading to many false positives, and
  offer limited mechanistic insight or interpretability.

* IMV  — *Integrated Motivational–Volitional Model* (O’Connor) [~2011]
  A multi-phase framework separating background vulnerability, ideation
  formation (via defeat and entrapment), and transition to action (via
  volitional factors like access, impulsivity, and exposure). It integrates
  many known risk factors into a coherent structure and has relatively strong
  empirical support, especially for entrapment. Its complexity makes full
  validation and clinical application more difficult.

* 3ST  — *Three-Step Theory* (Klonsky & May) [~2015]
  A streamlined ideation-to-action model: pain plus hopelessness generates
  ideation, low connectedness intensifies it, and capacity enables attempts. It
  improves on IPTS by clarifying the role of connectedness and simplifying
  interactions. Empirical support is reasonably consistent for ideation, but
  like other models, it struggles to robustly predict attempts.

* IPTS — *Interpersonal Theory of Suicide* (Joiner) [~2005]
  Proposes that perceived burdensomeness and thwarted belongingness produce
  suicidal desire, while acquired capability enables action. It is conceptually
  clear and clinically intuitive, with strong support for burdensomeness.
  However, its core interaction structure is inconsistently replicated, and the
  capability construct has weak measurement and predictive validity.

* Beck — *Cognitive Model of Suicide* (Aaron T. Beck) [~1970s]
  Emphasizes hopelessness, negative schemas, and cognitive constriction as
  drivers of suicidal thinking. It is tightly integrated with
  cognitive-behavioral therapy and has strong empirical support for
  hopelessness as a predictor of ideation. The model is less specific about how
  individuals transition from thoughts to attempts.

* CoP  — *Cry of Pain Model* (Williams) [~1990s]
  Frames suicidality as a response to perceived defeat, entrapment, and lack of
  rescue. It has been influential in shaping later models (especially IMV),
  particularly around the concept of entrapment. As a standalone model, it is
  less frequently tested and is largely subsumed into more comprehensive
  frameworks.

* FVT  — *Fluid Vulnerability Theory* [~2000s]
  Conceptualizes suicide risk as a dynamic system with a stable baseline and
  acute fluctuations triggered by stressors. It captures temporal instability
  and crisis dynamics better than static models, aligning with clinical
  observations. Empirical testing is more limited, and it is less precise in
  specifying mechanisms for attempts.


Most suicide models have limited predictive power due to structural constraints
rather than flawed theory. Suicide is a low base-rate, highly heterogeneous,
and temporally unstable outcome, which limits individual-level prediction. Core
risk factors (e.g., isolation, depression) have small effect sizes and
primarily predict ideation rather than attempts, while the transition to action
depends on proximal, situational variables (e.g., access, impulsivity) that are
difficult to measure and time. Constructs are noisy, pathways are nonlinear,
and outcomes vary. As a result, these models explain population patterns better
than they predict specific events.
