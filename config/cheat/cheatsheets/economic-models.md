# Classical

Core:
- Markets self-correct.
- Supply creates demand.

Recessions:
- Wage/price distortions.
- Temporary imbalances.

Solution:
- Let markets adjust.
- Avoid intervention.

Government:
- Minimal state.
- Balanced budgets.

Formula:
- "Supply creates its own demand" (Say's Law)

Intuition:
- The economy naturally returns to equilibrium.

---

# Keynesian

Core:
- Demand drives output and employment.

Recessions:
- Insufficient aggregate demand.
- Sticky wages/prices.

Solution:
- Fiscal stimulus.
- Monetary easing.

Government:
- Active stabilization role.

Formula:
- Y = C + I + G + (X - M)

Legend:
- C = consumption
- I = investment
- G = government spending
- X - M = net exports

Intuition:
- Economies can remain stuck in recession.

---

# New Classical

Core:
- Rational expectations.
- Markets usually clear efficiently.

Recessions:
- Real shocks.
- Policy distortions.

Solution:
- Predictable policy rules.
- Limit discretionary intervention.

Government:
- Often ineffective at stabilization.

Formula:
- expected_future ~= model_consistent_forecast

Intuition:
- People adapt behavior to anticipated policy.

---

# Monetarism

Core:
- Money supply drives inflation and nominal growth.

Recessions:
- Monetary instability.
- Poor central bank policy.

Solution:
- Stable money growth.
- Inflation targeting.

Government:
- Strong central bank, restrained fiscal policy.

Formula:
- MV = PY

Legend:
- M = money supply
- V = velocity
- P = price level
- Y = output

Intuition:
- Inflation is primarily a monetary phenomenon.

---

# Austrian

Core:
- Credit expansion distorts markets.

Recessions:
- Artificial booms caused by cheap credit.

Solution:
- Allow correction/liquidation.
- Avoid intervention.

Government:
- Strongly anti-central-bank.
- Libertarian orientation.

Formula:
- No central canonical equation.

Intuition:
- Artificially low interest rates create bubbles.

---

# Marxian

Core:
- Capitalism contains structural contradictions.

Recessions:
- Falling profits.
- Overaccumulation.
- Class conflict.

Solution:
- Structural economic transformation.

Government:
- Depends on tradition; generally anti-capitalist.

Formula:
- profit <- labor exploitation dynamics

Intuition:
- Capitalism naturally produces instability and inequality.

---

# Supply-Side

Core:
- Incentives determine growth.

Recessions:
- Excess taxation/regulation.

Solution:
- Tax cuts.
- Deregulation.

Government:
- Limited but pro-business.

Formula:
- tax_rate affects economic activity and revenue

Intuition:
- Production incentives matter more than demand stimulus.

---

# Modern Monetary Theory (MMT)

Core:
- Sovereign currency issuers cannot "run out of money."

Recessions:
- Underused labor/resources.

Solution:
- Large fiscal spending if inflation remains controlled.

Government:
- Strong fiscal role.

Formula:
- real_constraint = inflation
- not federal_budget_balance

Intuition:
- Inflation, not deficits, is the true limit.

---

# Post-Keynesian

Core:
- Financial systems are inherently unstable.
- Uncertainty matters.

Recessions:
- Debt cycles.
- Financial fragility.

Solution:
- Regulation.
- Demand stabilization.

Government:
- Active management and oversight.

Formula:
- instability increases during prolonged booms

Intuition:
- Stability itself can create instability (Minsky).

---

# Quick Comparison

Market self-corrects?
- Classical: yes
- Keynesian: not reliably
- Austrian: mostly yes
- Marxian: no
- MMT: not the key question

Main driver of recessions?
- Classical: rigidities
- Keynesian: low demand
- Monetarist: money instability
- Austrian: credit bubbles
- Marxian: structural contradictions
- Post-Keynesian: financial fragility

Most influential today?
- New Keynesian + Neoclassical synthesis
- Central banking heavily influenced by Monetarism

---

# Modern Consensus

Modern macroeconomics is mostly hybrid:
- markets usually work,
- expectations matter,
- monetary policy matters,
- financial crises are real,
- severe recessions may require intervention.

Most institutions today:
- Federal Reserve
- ECB
- IMF
- Treasury departments

operate with some mixture of:
- New Keynesian
- Neoclassical
- Monetarist

ideas.
